Why Soap Bubbles Matters Now

For 120 years, English readers have been denied access to one of the sharpest satirical voices of the early 20th century.

Ricarda Huch—seven-time Nobel Prize nominee, first woman elected to the Prussian Academy of Arts, praised by Thomas Mann as "the First Lady of German letters"—wrote Soap Bubbles in 1905 as "Three Jesting Tales." But beneath the light entertainment lies devastating social critique that feels startlingly contemporary.

Inside This Collection

1. The Life of Saint Wonnebald Pück

A thoroughly mediocre priest stumbles upward through the church hierarchy—from failed monk to incompetent abbot to scheming bishop—until he's eventually canonized as a saint. Huch's irony is devastating but never heavy-handed: she lets the absurdity of institutional corruption speak for itself. Sound familiar?

2. From Bimbo's Transmigrations

A soul's journey through various reincarnations, told with the kind of philosophical playfulness that rewards careful reading. Huch explores identity, consciousness, and what it means to be human—questions that feel especially urgent in our age of AI and technological transformation.

3. The Jewish Grave

When a Jewish merchant dies in a small provincial town, the community's elaborate schemes to deny him a proper burial expose the ugly machinery of religious intolerance. The humor is dark, the observation of human pettiness precise, and the critique unmistakable. Reading it today—with antisemitism resurgent and "othering" a daily political weapon—Huch's 1905 satire cuts deeper than ever.

Why These Stories Resonate in 2026

Huch understood that comedy could carry truths too uncomfortable for solemn treatment. Her targets in 1905—religious hypocrisy, institutional corruption, the casual cruelty toward outsiders—remain our targets today.

But there's something else that makes this translation urgent: Huch lived to see her satire become prophecy. Two years after becoming the first woman elected to the Prussian Academy of Arts in 1931, she resigned in protest when the Nazi regime demanded the expulsion of Jewish members. She spent the war years in internal exile, then devoted her final years to documenting German resistance.

That she could write with such wit about religious intolerance in 1905, and then live to oppose its most catastrophic expression, gives these "jesting tales" an unexpected weight. She knew exactly what she was satirizing, and history proved her right.

This is the first English translation.

After 120 years, English readers finally have access to one of the most brilliant satirical voices of the early 20th century—a writer who saw clearly, wrote beautifully, and stood firm when it mattered most.

Who Was Ricarda Huch?

A Forgotten Giant of German Literature

Ricarda Huch (1864-1947) was once one of Germany's most respected writers—the first woman admitted to the Prussian Academy of Arts, a prolific novelist, poet, and historian who resisted the Nazi regime and remained true to her principles throughout her life.

Thomas Mann called her "the first lady of Germany." Her contemporaries compared her to Goethe. Yet today, outside of Germany, her work is nearly unknown.

Soap Bubbles showcases her at her satirical best: witty observations of marriage, society, and the quiet rebellions of intelligent women trapped by convention.

About the Translator

Terra Laggner
Literary Translator

Recovering Forgotten Voices

Terra Laggner is a literary translator specializing in German-to-English translation of forgotten and overlooked works. Soap Bubbles represents her debut translation for Forgotten Voices Press—the culmination of years spent searching for German writers whose brilliance deserved a wider audience.

"I first encountered Ricarda Huch while looking for overlooked German writers whose work had never found its way into English," Terra explains. "Her name kept appearing—seven-time Nobel Prize nominee, first woman elected to the Prussian Academy of Arts, praised by Thomas Mann—and yet almost nothing of hers was available to English readers. That seemed like an injustice worth correcting."

Terra's approach to translation prioritizes both accuracy and readability. "Huch's German is elegant but never fussy," she notes. "I wanted English that moves with the same easy confidence—preserving the lightness and the edge without underlining the irony or dulling the wit."

Based in Eureka, California, Terra is currently working on additional translations of forgotten German literary voices for Forgotten Voices Press, with a particular focus on women writers from the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Our Mission

German literature from 1850-1954 is rich with forgotten voices—especially women writers, regional authors, and those whose work was overshadowed by political upheaval or changing tastes.

Forgotten Voices Press brings these literary treasures to English readers through premium, professionally-edited translations. We focus on works that deserve to be rediscovered: brilliant, moving, insightful books that time has unjustly forgotten.

Our goal: Build a catalog of 10-20 translations that restore these voices to the conversation they deserve to be part of.
