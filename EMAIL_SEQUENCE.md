# Email Sequence Templates

## Overview

This is a 4-email automated sequence designed to:
1. Deliver the sample chapter immediately
2. Build anticipation for the book launch
3. Tell the story of Ricarda Huch and why she matters
4. Convert subscribers into buyers on launch day

**Total sequence length:** 7-14 days (depending on your launch timeline)

---

```
**Ricarda Huch: A Life Against the Current**

Born in 1864, Ricarda Huch lived a life that would have been extraordinary in any era—but was nearly impossible for a woman of her time.

**She was the first woman to:**
- Earn a doctorate from a Swiss university (Germany wouldn't allow it)
- Join the Prussian Academy of Arts
- Write a comprehensive history of the Thirty Years' War that's still studied today

**She was also:**
- A novelist who explored women's psychology decades before it became fashionable
- A poet, essayist, and literary critic whose work influenced an entire generation
- A moral voice who resigned from the Academy in 1933 to protest Nazi cultural policies

Thomas Mann called her work "magnificent." Hermann Hesse praised her "intellectual independence." She was nominated for the Nobel Prize in Literature.

**So why haven't you heard of her?**

The same reason you haven't heard of dozens of brilliant women writers from the early 20th century: they were systematically overlooked, excluded from anthologies, and allowed to drift into obscurity after their deaths.

Huch died in 1947, just two years after the war ended. In the chaos of reconstruction, her literary reputation faded. By the 1960s, she was a footnote. By the 1980s, forgotten entirely—except in Germany, where scholars never stopped reading her.

**This translation is part of bringing her back.**

"Soap Bubbles" (1905) showcases Huch at her most playful and satirical. These three novellas are sharp, funny, psychologically acute, and delightfully readable.

They deserve an English-speaking audience.

Over my next email, I'll share what goes into translating a work like this—and why I believe literary translation is one of the most important (and undervalued) art forms.

**Until then: Keep reading.**

Best,
Alfred
```

## Email 3: Translation Philosophy

**Timing:** 7 days after Email 1 (4 days after Email 2)

**Goal:** Position this as a premium literary translation, not a quick conversion

### Subject Line Options:

- **Option B:** Lost (and found) in translation
- **Option C:** Why I spent 200 hours translating this book

**Preview Text:** The art of bringing forgotten voices across languages

### Email Body:

```
Here's something I learned while translating "Soap Bubbles":

Ricarda Huch was obsessed with language. Her sentences are musical. Her word choices are precise. Her dialogue captures distinct voices and social registers.

In other words, she's a translator's nightmare—and delight.

---

**What Makes a Great Literary Translation?**

Bad translations prioritize speed. They convert words from Language A to Language B as quickly as possible. The result reads like instructions for assembling furniture.

Good translations prioritize accuracy. They capture the meaning, preserve the structure, and convey the author's ideas faithfully.

**Great translations prioritize *reading experience*.**

They ask: Does this sound like something a talented writer would write *in English*? Does it flow naturally? Does it preserve the author's voice while feeling native to the target language?

That's what I aimed for with "Soap Bubbles."

**My Translation Approach:**

1. **First pass:** AI-assisted translation (Claude) to handle the heavy lifting of grammar and vocabulary
2. **Second pass:** Line-by-line human editing for style, voice, and nuance
3. **Third pass:** Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing
4. **Fourth pass:** Consistency check (names, terms, recurring phrases)
5. **Final pass:** Proofread by a second reader

**Total time:** Approximately 200 hours across 6 months.

This isn't a quick cash grab. It's a literary restoration project.

**Why It Matters:**

When you read "Soap Bubbles" in English, you should feel like you're reading a witty, sharp-eyed, psychologically astute novelist—not a machine translation of German sentences.

You should laugh at Huch's satire. You should recognize her characters. You should turn pages because the story pulls you forward, not because you're decoding awkward phrasing.

**That's the promise of this translation.**

I can't wait to share the full book with you.
```

## Personalization Tips

**Make it feel personal:**
- Use conversational tone (write like you're emailing a friend)
- Share personal insights about the translation process
- Encourage replies (and respond to them!)
- Use your real name and personality

**Don't do:**
- Overly promotional language ("Limited time offer! Act now!")
- Excessive exclamation points!!!
- Pushy sales tactics
- Long walls of text (break into short paragraphs)

**The best email marketing feels like a letter from a friend, not an ad.**
